The Mars Orbiter Mission (MOM), also known as Mangalyaan, is a notable space mission by the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO) that aimed to explore the Martian environment. Launched on November 5, 2013, MOM was India's first interplanetary mission and a significant milestone in the country's space exploration efforts.

The primary goal of MOM was to study the Martian surface, morphology, atmosphere, and mineralogy from orbit. It aimed to provide valuable data about Mars' surface and atmosphere, as well as capture images of the planet.

Sensors

The sensors aboard the Mangalyan included -

  • Alpha Photometer (LAP): LAP was designed to measure the relative abundance of deuterium and hydrogen in the Martian upper atmosphere. This information helps in understanding the history of water on Mars.
  • Mars Color Camera (MCC): MCC was the imaging instrument on board MOM, responsible for capturing high-resolution images of the Martian surface and its features.
  • Exospheric Neutral Composition Analyzer (MENCA): MENCA was used to analyze the neutral composition of the Martian exosphere.
  • Infrared Imaging Spectrometer (TIS): TIS was designed to study the Martian surface and mineralogy by measuring the thermal emissions from the planet.
  • Mars Imaging IR Spectrometer (MARS): MARS was another instrument for measuring the mineral composition of the Martian surface.
  • Methane Sensor for Mars (MSM): MSM aimed to detect traces of methane in the Martian atmosphere. Methane is of interest because it could be a sign of biological or geological activity.
  • These scientific instruments collectively allowed MOM to conduct a range of experiments and observations to study the Martian environment and surface.
